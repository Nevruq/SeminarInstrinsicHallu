Even if hallucination detection and mitigation techniques were to improve dramatically, the problem of intrinsic hallucination in LLMs cannot be fully eliminated — and this is not an engineering limitation, but a mathematical certainty. Banerjee et al. (2024) establish this through what they call "Structural Hallucinations": hallucinations that are an inherent part of the mathematical and logical structure of any LLM, present at every stage of the generation pipeline with a non-zero probability, regardless of architectural improvements or dataset enhancements.
The argument operates on five compounding layers of fundamental impossibility. First, no training dataset can ever be complete: using a Gödel-like self-referential construction, the authors prove that for any training database D, one can always construct a statement S_H — "there exist true facts beyond my training data" — which leads to a hallucination whether the statement is true or false, since the LLM either generates something unverifiable or something false. The incompleteness of training data is therefore not a contingent limitation of scale, but a logical inevitability. Second, even if the training data were complete, accurate information retrieval is undecidable: by reducing the Acceptance Problem — a classically undecidable problem in computability theory — to the "Needle in a Haystack" problem of LLMs, the authors formally prove that no algorithm can guarantee the correct retrieval of information from the model's knowledge base. Third, intent classification is likewise undecidable, as it reduces directly to the same undecidable retrieval problem. Fourth, and most fundamentally, LLM halting is undecidable: since transformer-based LLMs can simulate a Universal Turing Machine, the undecidability of the Halting Problem applies directly to them, meaning that an LLM cannot know a priori how many tokens it will generate, and therefore cannot check its own output before producing it. This means, as the paper states, that the LLM "is unable to predict its generation given a prompt" and hence "is unable to check its output prior to generation for accuracy or correctness." Fifth, and decisively for the question of mitigation: even post-generation fact-checking cannot eliminate hallucinations in a finite number of steps. The authors prove this by showing that a perfect finite-step fact-checker would imply the decidability of the Acceptance Problem — which is a known contradiction.
The deeper theoretical underpinning of all five proofs is Gödel's First Incompleteness Theorem, which states that any sufficiently powerful, consistent formal system contains true statements that cannot be proved within the system itself. An LLM, as a formal system operating over a finite parameter space, inherits this property: it cannot possess a complete and internally consistent representation of all truths, nor can it define a reliable internal truth predicate — a result further formalized by Tarski's Undefinability Theorem. The consequence is that there will always exist outputs that the model produces with apparent confidence and fluency, yet which contradict either the provided context (intrinsic hallucination) or external facts (extrinsic hallucination), and which no internal checking mechanism can catch with probability one. As Banerjee et al. summarize: "irrespective of how advanced our architectures or training datasets, or fact-checking guardrails may be, hallucinations are ineliminable." This does not mean that mitigation is pointless — detection methods, RAG, uncertainty quantification, and self-consistency approaches can all reduce the frequency of hallucinations significantly — but they operate asymptotically: they can push the probability of hallucination toward zero, but the mathematical structure of LLMs ensures it can never reach zero. The problem is therefore not one of engineering maturity, but of computability itself.

Statt ein möglicherweise unechtes Gödel-Zitat zu verwenden, wäre es akademisch sauberer, das Theorem selbst als Epigraph zu paraphrasieren, mit korrekter Quellenangabe:

"In any sufficiently powerful formal system, there exist true statements that cannot be proven within the system itself."
— Gödel's First Incompleteness Theorem (1931)

Und am Ende der Arbeit kann man dann elegant schließen:

Was Gödel für formale Systeme bewies, gilt auch für Large Language Models: Kein noch so mächtiges System kann vollständig und widerspruchsfrei alle Wahrheiten über sich selbst erfassen — und intrinsische Halluzination ist die unmittelbare Konsequenz dieser fundamentalen Grenze.

Kernprobleme im Überblick
Das Generalisierungsproblem: Orgad et al. zeigen, dass Fehlerdetektoren, die auf internen Repräsentationen trainiert werden, nicht über Datensätze hinweg generalisieren — ein auf einem Datensatz trainierter Detektor versagt oft auf einem anderen. Das bedeutet: LLM-basierte Detektion von intrinsischen Halluzinationen ist nicht robust.
Das Konfidenzproblem: Ein entscheidendes Signal in der Halluzinationsdetektion ist die eigene interne Unsicherheit des Modells — durch Analyse von Token-Level-Wahrscheinlichkeiten, Entropie und Konfidenz-Kalibrierung können Outputs identifiziert werden, bei denen das Modell unsicher ist. arXiv

LLMs sind für die Erkennung intrinsischer Halluzinationen begrenzt geeignet — und zwar aus strukturellen, nicht nur technischen Gründen. Die vielversprechendste Richtung ist die Nutzung interner Repräsentationen (Probing-Klassifikatoren auf Hidden States), da das Modell intern tatsächlich mehr "weiß" als es zeigt. Aber auch hier gilt: Der Detektor ist nicht universell, generalisiert schlecht, und kann das Grundproblem — das Banerjee et al. mathematisch bewiesen haben — nicht vollständig lösen. Ein LLM, das sich selbst auf Halluzinationen überprüft, ist letztlich ein formales System, das versucht, sein eigenes Wahrheitsprädikat zu definieren — genau das, was Tarski als unmöglich bewiesen hat.

However, dialogue systems allow relatively higher tolerance, since user engagement and conversational quality are also important goals, especially in open-domain settings.


prompt based approaches
\subsection{Prompt-Based LLM Detection}
Prompt-based detection, as used in the seed paper, uses LLMs directly as evaluators by instructing them to assess the faithfulness of a given output. Its primary advantage is is flexibility. No additional training is required, and the approach generalizes across tasks and languages. As \cite{Gogoulou2025LLMHallucinationDetection} demonstrate, well-prompted LLMs can perform competitive detection in a contrastive setting. However, as discussed in the previous section, this approach is subject to substantial prompt sensitivity, model-specific inconsistencies, and performance inflation due to the contrastive setup. The effectiveness of prompt-based detection therefore depends heavily on conditions that cannot be guaranteed in deployment.
